BASEL III CAPITAL ADEQUACY FRAMEWORK — SELECTED PROVISIONS
Synthetic Regulatory Excerpt for Workshop Use
Based on: Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, "Basel III: A global regulatory
framework for more resilient banks and banking systems" (Revised June 2011)

================================================================================
PART I: CAPITAL REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

Article 1 — Minimum Capital Requirements

1.1 Banks must maintain a minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio of 4.5% of
risk-weighted assets (RWA) at all times. This represents the highest quality of
regulatory capital, defined as common shares plus retained earnings minus
regulatory deductions.

1.2 The minimum Tier 1 capital ratio is set at 6.0% of RWA. Tier 1 capital
includes CET1 capital plus Additional Tier 1 (AT1) instruments that satisfy
the criteria in Article 4.

1.3 The minimum Total Capital ratio is set at 8.0% of RWA. Total Capital
includes Tier 1 capital plus Tier 2 capital instruments that satisfy the
criteria in Article 5.

1.4 In addition to the minimum requirements, banks are required to maintain
a capital conservation buffer of 2.5% of RWA, composed entirely of CET1
capital. Failure to maintain this buffer restricts distributions of earnings
and discretionary bonus payments as described in Article 8.

Summary of Minimum Capital Ratios:

  | Capital Component          | Minimum Ratio | Including Buffer |
  |----------------------------|---------------|-----------------|
  | Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1)| 4.5%          | 7.0%            |
  | Tier 1 Capital             | 6.0%          | 8.5%            |
  | Total Capital (Tier 1 + 2) | 8.0%          | 10.5%           |

================================================================================
PART II: COUNTERCYCLICAL CAPITAL BUFFER
================================================================================

Article 2 — Countercyclical Buffer Requirement

2.1 National supervisory authorities may require banks under their jurisdiction
to hold an additional countercyclical buffer of up to 2.5% of RWA during
periods of excessive credit growth. The purpose of the countercyclical buffer
is to ensure that banking sector capital requirements take account of the
macrofinancial environment in which banks operate.

2.2 The buffer rate is set by the relevant national authority based on:
  (a) The credit-to-GDP gap (actual ratio minus long-run trend)
  (b) Other indicators of credit cycle position deemed relevant by the authority
  (c) Guidance provided by the Basel Committee

2.3 Banks must monitor the buffer requirements of each jurisdiction in which
they have private sector credit exposures. The bank-specific countercyclical
buffer requirement is a weighted average of the buffer rates in place in
jurisdictions to which the bank has credit exposures.

2.4 When a buffer is reduced, whether due to a change in buffer rates or
because of a bank's credit portfolio evolving, there is no restriction on
the distribution of earnings.

================================================================================
PART III: LEVERAGE RATIO
================================================================================

Article 3 — Non-Risk-Based Leverage Constraint

3.1 The minimum Tier 1 leverage ratio is 3.0%, defined as Tier 1 capital
divided by total exposure. This requirement acts as a backstop to the
risk-based capital framework and constrains the buildup of excessive leverage.

3.2 The leverage ratio exposure measure includes:
  (a) On-balance sheet items, net of specific provisions and valuation
      adjustments
  (b) Derivative exposures, measured using the Standardised Approach for
      Counterparty Credit Risk (SA-CCR)
  (c) Securities financing transaction exposures
  (d) Off-balance sheet items, including unconditionally cancellable
      commitments at a credit conversion factor of 10%

3.3 Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs) are subject to an additional
leverage ratio buffer requirement equal to 50% of their G-SIB risk-weighted
higher loss absorbency requirement. For a bank with a 2.0% G-SIB surcharge,
this implies a leverage ratio buffer of 1.0%, resulting in a total minimum
leverage ratio of 4.0%.

PART IV: LIQUIDITY STANDARDS
================================================================================

Article 6 — Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)

6.1 The Liquidity Coverage Ratio requires banks to maintain an adequate stock
of unencumbered High Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) that can be converted to
cash to meet liquidity needs in a 30-day stress scenario.

6.2 The minimum LCR is 100%, defined as:

    LCR = (Stock of HQLA) / (Total Net Cash Outflows over next 30 days) ≥ 100%

6.3 HQLA are classified into two categories:
  Level 1 Assets (no haircut):
    - Coins and banknotes
    - Central bank reserves
    - Marketable securities guaranteed by sovereigns with 0% risk weight
  Level 2 Assets (subject to haircut):
    - Level 2A: 15% haircut (e.g., non-0% sovereign securities rated AA– or above)
    - Level 2B: 25–50% haircut (e.g., RMBS rated AA or above, corporate bonds rated A+)

6.4 Level 2 assets may not exceed 40% of the total HQLA stock (after haircuts).
Level 2B assets may not exceed 15% of HQLA stock (after haircuts).

Article 7 — Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)

7.1 The Net Stable Funding Ratio requires banks to maintain a stable funding
profile relative to the composition of their assets and off-balance sheet
activities. It is defined as:

    NSFR = (Available Stable Funding) / (Required Stable Funding) ≥ 100%

7.2 Available Stable Funding (ASF) is the weighted sum of a bank's capital
and liabilities. Higher weights are applied to more stable funding sources:
  - Tier 1 and Tier 2 capital: 100% ASF factor
  - Retail deposits with residual maturity ≥ 1 year: 95% ASF factor
  - Retail demand deposits and term deposits < 1 year: 90–95% ASF factor
  - Wholesale funding with residual maturity ≥ 1 year: 50–100% ASF factor

7.3 Required Stable Funding (RSF) reflects the liquidity characteristics of
assets. Illiquid assets require more stable funding:
  - Encumbered assets with residual maturity ≥ 1 year: 100% RSF factor
  - Unencumbered loans to non-financial corporates, residual maturity < 1 yr: 50%
  - Unencumbered high-quality HQLA Level 1: 5% RSF factor
  - Unencumbered HQLA Level 2A: 15% RSF factor

PART V: RISK-WEIGHTED ASSET CALCULATION
================================================================================

Article 8 — Standardised Approach to Credit Risk

8.1 Under the Standardised Approach (SA), banks assign a risk weight to each
on-balance sheet exposure based on the exposure class and, where permitted, the
external credit rating of the counterparty. Risk-weighted assets are calculated
as the product of the exposure value and the applicable risk weight.

    RWA = Exposure Value × Risk Weight
    Minimum Capital Required = RWA × 8.0%

8.2 Exposure Class Risk Weights
The following risk weights apply under the SA for credit risk:

  | Exposure Class                      | Risk Weight     | Notes                              |
  |-------------------------------------|-----------------|------------------------------------|
  | Central governments (AAA to AA–)    | 0%              | Domestic currency exposures        |
  | Central governments (A+ to A–)      | 20%             |                                    |
  | Central governments (BBB+ to BB–)   | 50%             |                                    |
  | Central governments (below BB–)     | 150%            |                                    |
  | Banks (AAA to AA–)                  | 20%             | Short-term: 20%                    |
  | Banks (A+ to BBB–)                  | 50%             | Short-term: 20–50%                 |
  | Corporate (AAA to AA–)              | 20%             |                                    |
  | Corporate (BBB+ to BBB–)            | 75%             |                                    |
  | Corporate (below BB–)               | 150%            |                                    |
  | Retail (regulatory retail portfolio)| 75%             | Granularity criteria must be met   |
  | Residential mortgage (LTV ≤ 60%)    | 20%             |                                    |
  | Residential mortgage (LTV 80–90%)   | 50%             |                                    |
  | Commercial real estate              | 100%            | Subject to national discretion     |
  | Non-performing loans                | 150%            | Net of specific provisions         |
  | Equity exposures                    | 100–250%        | Listed vs. unlisted                |

8.3 Credit Conversion Factors (CCF) for Off-Balance Sheet Items
Off-balance sheet exposures are converted to credit exposure equivalents before
applying risk weights. The CCF represents the fraction of the undrawn commitment
expected to be drawn at default.

  | Off-Balance Sheet Category                           | CCF  |
  |------------------------------------------------------|------|
  | Unconditionally cancellable commitments              | 10%  |
  | Short-term self-liquidating trade finance            | 20%  |
  | Medium/long-term commitments (residual maturity > 1yr)| 40%  |
  | Direct credit substitutes (e.g., financial guarantees)| 100% |
  | Performance bonds and warranties                    | 50%  |

8.4 Internal Ratings-Based Approach (IRB)
Banks approved by their national supervisor may use the Internal Ratings-Based
(IRB) approach instead of the SA. Under IRB, risk weights are derived from
bank-supplied estimates of:
  - PD (Probability of Default)
  - LGD (Loss Given Default) — Foundation IRB uses supervisory LGD values
  - EAD (Exposure at Default)
  - M (Effective Maturity)

The IRB risk weight formula produces a capital requirement that covers unexpected
losses at a 99.9% confidence level over a one-year horizon. Banks using the
Advanced IRB (A-IRB) approach may additionally estimate their own LGD and EAD.

PART VI: SYSTEMICALLY IMPORTANT BANKS
================================================================================

Article 9 — G-SIB Surcharge

9.1 Global Systemically Important Banks are subject to additional CET1 capital
surcharges based on their systemic importance score. The surcharge scale:

  | G-SIB Bucket | Systemic Importance Score | CET1 Surcharge |
  |--------------|--------------------------|----------------|
  | Bucket 5     | 530+                     | 3.5%           |
  | Bucket 4     | 430–529                  | 2.5%           |
  | Bucket 3     | 330–429                  | 2.0%           |
  | Bucket 2     | 230–329                  | 1.5%           |
  | Bucket 1     | 130–229                  | 1.0%           |

9.2 The systemic importance score is calculated using five equally weighted
indicator categories:
  (a) Cross-jurisdictional activity (20%)
  (b) Size (20%)
  (c) Interconnectedness (20%)
  (d) Substitutability / financial institution infrastructure (20%)
  (e) Complexity (20%)
